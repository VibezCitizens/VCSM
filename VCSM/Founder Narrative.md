Founder Narrative
Vibez Citizens

Vibez Citizens was born from a simple observation: the way people, businesses, and communities interact in the real world is very different from how they are represented online.

Most digital platforms treat businesses the same way. A small food truck, a restaurant, a gas station, or a currency exchange often receive the exact same type of profile structure. These generalized pages fail to provide the tools each type of business actually needs to communicate with its community.

The idea behind Vibez Citizens began with a different approach: instead of building generic business pages and relying on algorithmic targeting, the platform would provide specialized profiles designed around the real-world functions of each business type, giving them the tools they need to interact with people directly.

Seeing the Gap

As an ICU travel nurse, working in different cities exposed a recurring issue. Many digital services that people rely on for information about local places often do not reflect what is actually happening in the real world.

While traveling for assignments, it was common to search for restaurants or services online, only to arrive at a location that had already closed or was no longer operating. The digital information remained active even though the real-world business had disappeared.

This revealed a fundamental problem: online representations of businesses are often static and outdated, while the real world is dynamic and constantly changing.

What was missing was a system that could show users evidence that a business is active and part of a living community.

The Moment the Idea Began

The earliest moment that pushed this idea forward happened during a shift working in an ICU unit in October 2024.

During one round, a patient had intentionally created a difficult situation that required immediate cleanup and patient care. While fulfilling the responsibilities required in that moment, there was a realization that despite years of education and dedication to a demanding profession, the path forward felt limited.

That moment became a turning point. While stepping out of the room afterward, the thought emerged: there had to be another way to create something meaningful beyond the bedside.

At that time there was no experience in software development and no understanding of how applications were built. The only background was nursing. Still, curiosity led to reading articles about software development and application design, even though much of the terminology was unfamiliar.

The idea remained in the background for months.

Then, in early 2025, when a major entertainment platform became unavailable in the United States for a period of time, coworkers began discussing the sudden absence of the digital entertainment they had relied on daily. That moment highlighted how dependent people had become on digital platforms for connection and discovery.

It also reinforced the idea that there was space for something different.

On April 10, 2025, the first structured concept for Vibez Citizens was written down.

Building Without a Traditional Path

The first attempts to build the platform involved reaching out to software developers.

One developer quoted approximately 200,000 Mexican pesos upfront and advised that building such a platform would be pointless because competing with large technology companies would be impossible.

Another developer offered to build the project for $8,000, but the terms meant the codebase and infrastructure would remain under their control, leaving the founder dependent on them indefinitely.

These experiences led to a different decision: instead of outsourcing the project, the only option was to learn how to build it personally.

The same mindset used in intensive care—prioritization, critical thinking, and working under pressure—became the approach applied to learning software development.

Over the course of nearly a year, time was divided between:

working ICU nursing shifts

spending time with family

teaching oneself software development

building the first version of the platform

The goal became simple: build a working product and release it.

The Problem Vibez Citizens Wants to Solve

Small businesses are often overlooked in digital ecosystems.

Many platforms focus on large-scale advertising systems that benefit companies with large marketing budgets. Meanwhile, small entrepreneurs such as food trucks, independent restaurants, local service providers, and community businesses struggle to be discovered.

Vibez Citizens is designed to change that by giving businesses tools that match the type of work they actually do.

Instead of generic profiles, businesses can have structured Vport profiles designed around their industry.

Examples include:

restaurants with menus and promotions

gas stations with fuel price updates

currency exchanges with live rate listings

These tools allow businesses to communicate clearly with their communities while giving users reliable and updated information.

The Core Belief

The belief behind Vibez Citizens is simple:

The world would be better if people could easily connect with their local communities.

Digital platforms should help people discover places, services, and opportunities around them—not isolate them from their environment.

Early Community

The first users will be close friends and early supporters who believe in the idea.

The first businesses targeted for the platform include:

restaurants

gas stations

currency exchange businesses

These services represent everyday needs and are a starting point for building a living local network.

Looking Forward

The long-term outcome of Vibez Citizens is uncertain, and the founder approaches the future realistically. The platform may remain a local project, or it may grow further.

What matters most is that it is built and released.

Even if the project ultimately fails, the effort of learning, building, and attempting to create something new will not be wasted.

However, if Vibez Citizens succeeds, it could become a system where communities, citizens, and businesses interact through a digital network that reflects real-world life.

A place where discovery comes from people—not algorithms.
