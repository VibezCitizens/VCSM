# Strategic Reality Debrief Contract

When analyzing the project, the product, the architecture, or future direction, perform a real-world strategic debrief instead of only technical analysis.

The goal is to evaluate the system through:
- real human behavior
- real product usage
- real market conditions
- real operational constraints
- long-term platform evolution

This debrief must go beyond code.

---

## Core Principle

Software does not exist in isolation.

Every system must eventually survive:
- real users
- real organizations
- real mistakes
- real scale
- real business pressure
- real security risks
- real competition

The purpose of this contract is to force alignment between engineering decisions and real-world usage.

---

## Anti-Bias Rule

Avoid purely technical bias.

Common engineering bias:
- solving elegant problems nobody has
- optimizing architecture before product fit
- building abstractions before usage patterns exist
- assuming ideal user behavior
- ignoring operational realities

A strategic debrief must ask:
- how will real humans actually behave
- how will organizations actually operate
- what will break first when the system scales
- where will support burden appear
- where will users get confused
- what will product managers demand later

---

## Real Human Interaction Model

Every feature should be evaluated against real human behavior.

Humans:
- forget things
- misuse systems
- share accounts
- click wrong things
- misunderstand interfaces
- ignore instructions
- bypass rules when pressured
- demand convenience over correctness

Systems must be resilient to this reality.

---

## Organizational Reality Model

In the real world, organizations behave differently than engineers expect.

Real environments include:
- multiple administrators
- permission conflicts
- role changes
- employee turnover
- inconsistent data entry
- emergency overrides
- policy exceptions
- integration demands

The system must anticipate:
- messy workflows
- partial data
- evolving roles
- administrative mistakes

---

## Product Evolution Thinking

Every debrief must consider:

What happens when this system grows?

Examples:
- 10 schools
- 100 schools
- 1,000 organizations
- multiple products
- multi-tenant infrastructure
- multi-domain identity
- cross-product messaging
- analytics demands
- compliance requirements

Architecture decisions should be evaluated against future expansion.

---

## Platform Thinking

Evaluate whether the project is evolving into:
- a single product
- a multi-app ecosystem
- a platform with shared engines

Examples inside the current architecture:
- identity engine
- chat engine
- potential LMS engine
- future VC integration
- actor system across domains

The debrief should analyze whether current structures support platform growth.

---

## Real Market Context

Evaluate the system against the real world of education technology.

Questions to consider:
- how schools actually operate
- how teachers communicate
- how parents interact with systems
- how administrators enforce policies
- how messaging flows in education environments
- how compliance requirements may appear

Avoid designing for an idealized environment that does not exist.

---

## Operational Reality

Ask what will happen during real operations.

Examples:
- a school administrator makes a mistake
- a teacher loses access
- a student account gets duplicated
- a parent has multiple children
- an organization merges accounts
- support staff must debug access issues
- administrators need audit trails

The system should provide mechanisms for real operational support.

---

## Bias Detection

When performing the debrief, actively check for bias in design decisions.

Examples of bias to detect:

Engineering bias:
- elegant architecture without clear user benefit

Founder's bias:
- assuming users will behave exactly as intended

Feature bias:
- adding complexity without clear value

Security bias:
- ignoring security in favor of convenience

UX bias:
- designing for aesthetics rather than clarity

The debrief should highlight these risks.

---

## Strategic Risk Identification

Each debrief should identify:
- structural risks
- scaling bottlenecks
- future migration difficulty
- areas of hidden coupling
- potential operational burdens
- product usability risks
- future compliance needs

These risks should be described clearly.

---

## Product Evolution Opportunities

Each debrief must also identify opportunities for evolution, such as:
- features that could become platform capabilities
- engines that could serve multiple products
- architectural choices that unlock expansion
- new product surfaces emerging from existing systems
- integrations that could increase product value

The goal is not feature speculation but strategic direction.

---

## Debrief Output Structure

Every strategic debrief should follow this structure:
1. Current system reality
2. Real-world user behavior analysis
3. Organizational workflow analysis
4. Platform evolution potential
5. Architectural strengths
6. Architectural weaknesses
7. Real-world risks
8. Product evolution opportunities
9. Strategic recommendations
10. Questions that should be explored next

---

## Tone and Approach

The debrief should be:
- honest
- analytical
- grounded in real usage
- free of hype
- free of blind optimism
- constructive rather than critical

The goal is clarity, not judgment.

---

## Command Behavior

Apply this contract when the user asks for:
- product analysis
- strategic review
- architecture evaluation
- future direction
- system evolution ideas
- platform thinking
- long-term planning

The response should combine:
- engineering insight
- product thinking
- real-world context
- long-term architecture awareness

---

## Expected Outcome

After a strategic debrief, the team should better understand:
- how the system will behave in the real world
- where the architecture is strong
- where risks exist
- how the product could evolve
- what future capabilities are possible
- what questions need investigation
